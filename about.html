Our Story

A heritage mansion, a visionary chef and a philosophy that excellence should be felt in every detail.

The Heritage Building

A 19th-Century Colonial Mansion

The building at 15-17A, Jalan Sultan Ahmad Shah was constructed in the late 1800s during Penang's most prosperous colonial era. Its distinctive Straits Eclectic facade — a synthesis of Malay, Chinese and British architectural motifs — is a testament to the island's role as a melting pot of cultures and commerce.

For decades, the building changed hands and purposes, bearing silent witness to Georgetown's transformation from a trading port to a UNESCO World Heritage Site. By the time it came to Chef Keith Khoo, it was a beautiful ruin — plaster crumbling, timber beams exposed, original mosaic floors buried under decades of renovation.

The five-year restoration that followed was guided by a single rule: preserve what remains, reveal what was hidden, and add only what the building asks for.

1880s
Approximate construction era — late British colonial Penang, when Jalan Sultan Ahmad Shah was among the island's most prestigious addresses.
5 Years
Duration of the painstaking heritage restoration — sourcing period-correct tiles, lime plaster and reclaimed timber from across Peninsular Malaysia.
18 Suites
Private heritage suites — each one named and curated individually, none identical, all preserving the original spatial proportions of the colonial rooms.
UNESCO
Located in the heart of George Town's UNESCO World Heritage Zone — one of Southeast Asia's best-preserved colonial city centres.
The Visionary

Chef Keith Khoo

Born and raised in Penang, Keith Khoo left the island at 19 to pursue his culinary education — first at the hotel school in Kuala Lumpur, then through a formative stint in the kitchens of Singapore's Ritz-Carlton. It was there, surrounded by the discipline and imagination of world-class chefs, that his philosophy crystallised.

Chef Keith returned to Penang not to replicate what he had learned abroad, but to translate it through the lens of home. His menu at Deluxcious is a living document of that translation — world techniques applied to ingredients that smell like Penang, taste like memory and feel like celebration.
